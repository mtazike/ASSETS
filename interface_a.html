What you are describing is tough, but also very common, especially in demanding graduate seminars where the workload is heavy and loosely structured. The situation is not fixed yet, but it will keep getting worse if you try to just push through without changing anything. The next step is not trying harder; it is changing your approach and getting support in place.

Start by talking to someone in your program now, not later. That could be your seminar professor, your advisor, or even a graduate coordinator. You do not need to overshare, but being honest that you are falling behind and trying to get back on track can open doors: extensions, prioritization advice, or even just clarity on what matters most. Professors usually respond better to early communication than silence followed by late work.

At the same time, you need to externalize structure, because ADHD makes it much harder to rely on internal motivation and memory. Break readings and assignments into very small, concrete steps and schedule them in a way that is hard to ignore. Instead of "do the reading," try "read 5 pages at 10am" or "write 3 bullet points at 2pm." It may feel overly simple, but that is the point: reduce friction.

You should also look into campus support. Most universities have disability or accessibility services that can provide accommodations for ADHD, such as extended deadlines, note-taking support, or flexible pacing. If you are not already connected with them, that is a practical step that can make a real difference.

Finally, challenge the assumption that falling behind means you cannot succeed. Right now you are reacting to a system that does not fit how you work best. With the right adjustments, including support, structure, and communication, you can still turn this around. Waiting and hoping it fixes itself is the one option that almost guarantees it will not.
